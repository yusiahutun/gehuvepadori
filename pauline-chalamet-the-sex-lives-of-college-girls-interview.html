Pauline Chalamet Knows That Sex in College Isnt About Just Sex

May 25, 2024
blog

Let’s get this out of the way first: Yes, Pauline Chalamet is Timothée Chalamet’s sister. The actress (who’s also a director, a sometime musician, and a former dancer) shares her famous younger sibling’s elfin features and bohemian New York City background. But while Timothée is off exploring the deserts of Arrakis, Pauline’s new project is a lot closer to home: She stars in Mindy Kaling’s new HBO Max comedy, The Sex Lives of College Girls.

Chalamet plays Kimberly Finkle, a rule-following valedictorian whose high school experience in small-town Arizona has not remotely prepared her for life at fancy Essex College; her performance is funny, frank, and instantly winning. You can practically see the gears in Kimberly’s head spinning as she tries to adjust to life at a prestigious college, with all its new people (many jokes are made about the lack of diversity in her hometown), socioeconomic unfairness (she has a work-study job, unlike most of her classmates), and confusingly hot guys. As Chalamet puts it, The Sex Lives of College Girls isn’t about just sex—it’s about everything that happens before, after, and because of it.

Chalamet has worked on short films and TV in the United States and France (her father is French), and she had a part in Judd Apatow’s The King of Staten Island, but this is her first major Hollywood role. Below, she talks to BAZAAR.com about everything from America’s obsession with sex to the French concept of time to her own arts-infused childhood in New York City.

What drew you to the role of Kimberly?

I thought it was a very well-written pilot; there were so many funny elements in it. When I read the script, I was like, “I hope they’re asking me to read for Kimberly.” There were so many in-between moments to explore with her.

What do you mean by “in between”?

Well, there’s what she says and what she’s reacting to, but in between that, there’s all the interior monologues that are happening.

Yeah, you can tell that she’s learning and changing very quickly—that she’s coming from this sort of sheltered place and that every encounter she has, she’s growing and becoming less naive. Was that fun for you as an actor?

It really was! It was fun to go back and explore what it was like to be a freshman in college again, but through the eyes of Kimberly who feels out of place socially and economically, in a structure that’s so new for her.

You went to Bard, right?

I did.

Did it feel like Essex?

It’s a very small school and it’s not a university, but it certainly is a private institution like Essex. And I definitely was able to relate to feeling out of place, especially economically. You know, you’re not really close to the people you first meet at college. You’re living together, so you kind of become close very quickly, but not close in your heart. You’re not letting them in on your insecurities. So that felt very, very familiar: feeling like you need to fit in, but then also knowing that you don’t.

The show has an almost all-female writers’ room, and it’s devoted to talking about all sorts of experiences of female sexuality. Do you think there’s a gap that it’s trying to fill?

Well, the United States has these Puritan origins, and we have a sex fetish, right? There’s just something about women and sex. I think the show is very of its time. It’s depicting these four young women who are navigating college, and there’s sex, but what happens before sex and after sex is a big part of it.

For so long, shows that deal with sexuality—especially female sexuality—have been about what happens under the sheets. So if there’s a gap the show is trying to fill, it’s being about more than that. It’s about the texts that come before, it’s the behavior of both partners (or more) after, it’s the moment of intimacy. We’re not naked in every scene, but so much of what’s going on is a mirror of the society in which we live, which is a sex-obsessed society. Even though we tried to pretend it’s not. [Laughs.]

The name of the show feels prurient in a way, like, Ooooh, sexy college girls.

I read the title and I’m like, “Oh, my God. It’s about banging girls who just have amazing sex. They’re having orgasms left and right. They have all these different partners and they’re exploring everything.” But I don’t know anyone—or very few people—who at that age were able to do that. Most people I knew were like, “What do I do? Am I straight? What else is out there? Do I even like sex??” It’s like, “Who even likes sex in the beginning? And why are we always being sold this image of people who know exactly what they want?” I think that’s part of what we’re trying to answer.

You’ve done all sorts of different kinds of creative work: You’ve directed, you’ve acted, you were a dancer growing up. Do you have a favorite?

I want to continue with all of them. I mean, not so much the dancing—the dancing was really put to bed, but right now, I want to focus on acting. The way I came to it was very roundabout. I was exploring all these different things; I had so many odd jobs. And then I came back full circle, like, “No, I want to be in the arts.”

What was your weirdest odd job?

I was a copy editor. I loved it. I love grammar. I’m obsessed. I was a bartender. I worked in a cafe. I was a dog walker. I was a babysitter. I was a tutor. Once I was asked to half-babysit, half-bartend. The parents were like, “Set up all the alcohol there on that table, you can bartend, but also watch these eight kids running around.”

You’re bilingual in French, right? Do you feel like you’ve been influenced—as an artist or a person—by having a French side?

Yeah, I’m bilingual. I spent a little bit of my childhood in France, and I wanted to get back there. I like the structure of French society. I feel very comfortable living in this society that has a lot of rules. Like how you have to set the table before you eat, or the dynamics of who you’re inviting over to your home, the way you greet people. You kind of know how to act. There’s less room for awkwardness.

What was it like growing up in New York in such a creative family?

Well, I grew up in Hell’s Kitchen, right next to Times Square, in a subsidized arts building. It was kooky as all hell, but it was my home. You know, when you’re a child, it all feels normal. And then, you leave and you realize how different it is. It was like growing up in a musical. You’d walk into an elevator and somebody would be on their way to an audition and they’d be practicing scales. So it made the idea of pursuing a career in the arts feel normal to me, and for that, I’m really grateful.

When did you realize that this was an unusual childhood?

Probably when I brought a friend home from college. We were waiting for the elevator, and these two people were standing in two different elevators, and as the elevator doors were closing, they were singing a duet. And my friend was like, “What?”

Tell me about the indie movie you made, What Doesn’t Float.

It’s in post-production. Currently, as we speak, there is a live mini orchestra that’s recording the score for it. What Doesn't Float is really a labor of love. We’re all friends from high school, and we’ve all worked on each other’s short films, and then we were like, “Let’s make a feature.” Basically, it’s seven vignettes about different New Yorkers as they enter into conflict in different worlds of New York. I’m really proud of what it’s going to be.
