1. The Owl of Athena (Little Owl)

Who is the God of owls? When you seek an answer to this question, the name Owl of Athena easily pops up. Athena is the goddess of war and wisdom in Greek mythology. The owl is regarded as a symbol of knowledge, wisdom, perspicacity and erudition throughout the Western world.

As the story goes, Athena's owl's name was originally a woman named Nyctimene. She lived as a princess on the island of Lesbos. Nyctimene was horribly abused by her own father, and she was forced to flee into the wilderness to escape her abuser. Athena took pity on her and turned her into an owl so that she could be her companion.

5. Barn owl

This is the most common owl in the world. It can easily be distinguished from other owls for its white, heart-shaped face. 6. Cikap-Kamuy

The Ainu people of Japan have a god named Cikap-kamuy (also called Kotan-kor-kamuy). He is believed to oversee the behaviour of humans. As the story goes, famine had struck the land, and humankind was starving. Chikap Kamuy wished to send a message to heaven inquiring about the cause of the famine. He sent two messengers, the Dipper Bird and Crow, who fell asleep.

Cikap-Kamuy grew angry and killed both of them. The third messenger was the Dipper Bird, who flew to heaven and returned with the news that the kamuy were angry because humans had stopped showing proper respect for the gifts they gave. Cikap Kamuy went to the humans and taught them the proper rituals to appease the kamuy, and the famine ceased.

Gwydion was a trickster god in Welsh folklore. He often tried to escape the punishment for his crimes or get what he wanted. Gwydion was the son of Don and the nephew and heir of Math, king of Gwynedd.

Lilith is one of the most ancient goddesses. She is a figure in Jewish mythology who is often associated with being a demon. A popular story about her origin says she was Adam's first wife and was created in God's image, just like Adam. However, she was thrown out of the garden of Eden after she disobeyed Adam.

She hid in a cave and mated with the fallen angels, and spawned a generation of demons. It is said that three angels discovered her hideout and demanded that she return to the Garden of Eden. 14. Sheela na Gig

If you are looking for fantasy owl names, Sheela na Gig should be at the top of your list. Throughout parts of ancient Celtic lands, sculptures of the Sheela Na Gig are present in churches and other buildings.

These figurative carvings are depicted as naked women serving the same purpose as gargoyles in modern churches. The carvings often have a resemblance to owls, which further cements the connection between women and owls in Celtic culture.

16. Cherokee owl

Traditional Cherokee Indians are leery of many owls. There are three different types of owls that are mentioned in Cherokee folklore, and they all symbolize different things. For example, the screech owl was believed to be a messenger of things to come.

The most well-known owl in Cherokee culture was Tsligi. This owl was believed to be an evil omen and was even associated with witches and dark forces.

17. Lechuza

Lechuza is an unnaturally large owl with a wingspan of about 15 feet and the face of a woman. The legend of La Lechuza is rooted in Mexican folklore. Stories of the lechuza are thought to have been around since the Spanish colonized Mexico. It is said that a lechuza is a bruja or witch that has taken the form of this owl.

Some say it is a white owl, but there are stories and reports that it is a black owl. As the story goes, Lechuza was once a woman who was wronged and now seeks revenge.
