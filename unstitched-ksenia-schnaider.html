Unstitched: Ksenia Schnaider

July 19, 2024
blog

When it comes to sustainable fashion, it can be hard to know where to start. To help, every month we will focus on a brand that knows exactly what it means to be a sustainable force for good in fashion today.

This month we’re getting to know Ksenia Schnaider, an upcycled denim brand that transforms used materials into statement-making pieces.

Although jeans are a necessity in most everyday wardrobes, creating denim can be very harmful to the planet; it takes around 1,800 gallons of water to grow enough cotton to create just one pair of jeans. During this process, toxic dyes and other harmful chemicals make their way into the oceans and harm both human health and the environment.

Andrew Grey

Upcycling is one eco-friendly alternative to shopping for your next pair of jeans or denim jacket. It focuses on the premise of making the most of what we already have; turning old fabrics or secondhand pieces into something new. Many brands are now embracing upcycling, with Ukrainian label Ksenia Schnaider being one of them, demonstrating just how creative the process can be.

Over the past decade, the brand has embodied what it means to be eco-conscious in the denim industry, through its designs that feature patchwork pieces, leftover knitting, secondhand vintage sportswear, and deadstock tapestry. The result? Statement-making staple pieces that are guaranteed to spark joy not just for the wearer, but for those who see you wearing them.

Sophie Turner wearing Ksenia Schnaider

Founded in 2011 by husband and wife duo Ksenia and Anton Schnaider, the brand is instantly recognisable for its signature cuts, techniques and attention to detail, which is all created at the brand's own production house in Kyiv. Over the years the brand has grown in popularity and has been worn by the likes of Sophie Turner, Ellie Goulding, Dua Lipa and the Hadid sisters.

We caught up with one-half of the label, Ksenia, to discover what she's learned during her decade at the helm of the brand and why we should all be re-wearing as much as possible.

Courtesy of Ksenia Schnaider

Ksenia and Anton Schnaider

What makes a truly sustainable brand?

Ksenia Schnaider: "I think this is a path without an ending, as we can always be improving. To become truly sustainable, you must at least start moving along this path. It's about making important changes every day – not just for what looks good on the surface or in press releases."

How can denim brands, in particular, be more responsible?

"We all know how much water is used in the production of denim and how many chemicals are involved to industrially wash jeans. You can start by choosing less harmful options in the production process. The best way for me to do this is to rework vintage jeans into contemporary designs, which is what we do with our brand."

Andrew Grey

What do you think needs to change in the fashion industry?

"I know this is a utopia, but nevertheless, I would like clear rules to be developed to regulate and protect ideas. I see so much plagiarism; it's especially frustrating when big businesses and well-known names borrow ideas from local independent designers who are trying to make the industry a better place."

Why is upcycling so important for being more responsible with how we consume fashion?

"Because we already have enough clothes on the planet. We can stop producing tonnes of new pieces every day and upcycle what we have. I'm glad that upcycling is a huge and still growing trend now, so perhaps in the future we will reach a point when at least 50 per cent of each person's wardrobe would be upcycled."

Do you think there is a stigma against upcycling and secondhand fashion?

"At the beginning of starting out our brand around five to eight years ago, I noticed a stigma against upcycling and secondhand fashion. Now, the attitude has changed dramatically, from disgust to complete acceptance and love. It's great to see."

Andrew Grey

What do you want to achieve personally with your brand, in terms of sustainability?

"Our brand is now facing big challenges – a terrible war is going on in our country, so we had to say goodbye to many of our plans and completely rebuild our strategy. We planned to switch to renewable energy in our own production in Kyiv, so now we are doing everything possible to keep things the way it was before the war.

"Unfortunately, the war sets very different priorities. Now I'm focused more on my team and I'm doing everything to keep them safe and to give them the possibility to continue to work in our company. Now, it's important for me to continue to release collections and keep the team together."

Ksenia Schnaider

How else can shoppers support Ukrainian brands like yourself, in addition to ordering?

"It's important not only to financially support Ukrainian talents, which is vital for us now, but it's also equally as important to talk about Ukrainian businesses on your social networks – tag brands, and support their content. Share with others that you are wearing clothes made in a country in which there's a war. After all, almost all Ukrainian designers produce locally (even now) – they need this awareness and need to feel appreciated in order to stay strong and continue to carry on their work."

Do you think our attitudes to fashion have changed over the decade since you started your brand?

"When I started, there was a completely different attitude to upcycling. Around eight years ago, no one was altering old clothes on an industrial scale. When we started doing this, we decided not to declare it loudly because we didn't want to confuse our customers. We just altered clothes and wanted people to buy them for their design. Now, cool design isn't enough for shoppers and it's important for people to invest in upcycling and sustainability."

What advice would you give to those wanting to make their business greener?

"Reconsider your waste and leftovers. Ask yourself if it can be recycled or upcycled and given a second life. I think it's a great creative challenge to keep inventing ways to upcycle waste into something useful or beautiful."

Ksenia Schnaider

What’s the smallest change a consumer could make to become more eco-conscious?

"There is one simple rule that everyone doesn't like – stop and think when you buy something. Take a break, think for a few days; do you really need this thing and will you wear it more than 20, 50 times? If yes, then go ahead. And if the answer is no, then it is better to refrain from buying."

What’s the biggest misconception about sustainable clothing?

"A few years ago, I would say that the biggest misconception was that sustainable equals boring. Now I would say that sustainable equals expensive. I think it's time to stop comparing quality clothes to fast fashion and conclude that they are expensive. I'm sure clothes can't cost as much as a weekday lunch. We must respect innovation and hard work and pay reasonable prices for it."

Andrew Grey

Where do you turn to when you feel confused about sustainability and need more detail?

"I usually study the issue myself and search for the maximum information possible, or I ask my suppliers, manufacturers and colleagues. I try to educate myself on this topic – read and listen to Dana Thomas, listen to Business of Fashion podcasts, and visit different themed events and seminars."
