'The Garden of Time' by JG Ballard inspired this year's Met Gala

September 08, 2024
blog

The first Monday in May is fast approaching, which can mean only one thing: the return of the annual Met Gala, often referred to as the 'Fashion Oscars'. Taking place this year on 6 May, the fundraiser is in aid of, and held at, the Costume Institute – the fashion and textiles arm of the Metropolitan Museum in New York.

Every year, the gala is themed around a corresponding exhibition which, for 2024, is called Sleeping Beauties: Reawakening Fashion (you can learn more about the exhibition, its inspiration and what it will feature in our piece here). The theme for the event itself, meanwhile, is The Garden of Time – inspired by the J.G. Ballard short story of the same name. But what does the theme actually mean, and what is the original book about? Here's what we know about the gala's dress code – and how we expect guests to interpret it on the night...

Where does the Met Gala's 'The Garden of Time' theme come from?

Bettmann//Getty Images

Author J.G. Ballard, whose short story is the basis for this year’s Met Gala dress code

This year's dress code for the Met Gala is based on The Garden of Time, a 1962 short story by J.G. Ballard, an English novelist, satirist and essayist known for his provocative works that test the relationship between human psychology and mass media.

He first became famous for his science fiction (for example, the post-apocalyptic work The Drowned World) but went on to become a household name for his war novel, Empire of the Sun, in 1984 – a semi-autobiographical story about the experiences of a British boy during the Japanese occupation of Shanghai. Three years later, Steven Spielberg adapted the novel into a film of the same name starring Christian Bale, to a phenomenal reception.

What is The Garden of Time about?

Ballard's short stories explore the same dystopian modernity as his longer fictions, and The Garden of Time is no exception. The story is about the good-looking, sophisticated Count Axel and his wife, who live in a beautiful villa full of rare books, fine paintings, busts and vases. They live a decadent life on their beautiful estate, which includes an exquisite pool and garden. But outside the walls, the landscape is empty as far as the eye can see – until an unruly rabble appears in the distance. Each time Count Axel cuts a flower from his garden, however, time spools back, and the brutish mass recedes behind the hills.

But a long time has passed since plants last bloomed, and the Count is reduced to cutting the very last of his flowers to hold back the mob. When their garden is bare, he and his wife carefully put away their books, their music and all the treasures of their civilised life, preparing for it to come to an abrupt end.

The vivid, thought-provoking short story is often seen to act as a metaphor for the evolution of human history and the endless cycle of creation and destruction — a theme clearly mirrored in The Met's Sleeping Beauties exhibition.

How will guests interpret the theme?

Given the obvious references to the natural world, we can expect to see plenty of floral motifs in this year's red-carpet looks – Simone Rocha's all-black gown, with flowers and long stems for Jean Paul Gaultier's spring 2024 haute couture collection, would be an ideal homage to the theme. Other couture shows will also surely provide ample inspiration, from Valentino's outsize florals to the more traditional, romantic blooms paraded down the catwalk at Giambattista Valli.

Of course, some guests may stray towards the darker end of the spectrum, embracing the sense of transience made evident in the theme, and in the book – from the inevitable doom of the aristocracy to the inability of money and beauty to halt death. For this, the recent Maison Margiela couture show, based on the degeneration seen on Parisian streets, would be a perfect fit. Alternatively, guests dipping into fashion archives would do well to explore Alexander McQueen, whose fascination with the macabre often riffed on death and decay, especially in relation to nature.
