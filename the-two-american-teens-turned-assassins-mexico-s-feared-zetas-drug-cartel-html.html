The two American teens turned assassins for Mexico's feared Zetas drug cartel

March 08, 2024
blog

Texas teens Gabriel Cardona and Rosalio 'Bart' Reta carried out bloody murders for a vicious and fearsome drug cartel in Mexico.

Amid the ongoing drug war in Mexico, the childhood friends who grew up in Texas border towns were lured into a life of violence, drugs and cash with the notorious Zetas cartel.

They were paid a $500 retainer each week, lived in a large house in Laredo, drove expensive cars and were paid up to $10,000 commission for each killing.

However, their short bloody careers came crashing down one after the other, with Cardona sentenced to 80 years behind bars while Reta got 70 years in prison.

But how they went from a good student and star quarterback to brutal assassins is revealed in Wolf Boys: Two American Teenagers and Mexico's Most Dangerous Drug Cartel, a new book by former Wall Street Journal reporter Dan Slater.

Since Cardona and Reta were born to families with relatives on both sides of the U.S.-Texas border, the ease at which they could cross back and forth across the border made them valuable to the ruthless Zetas cartel.

A poor kid from Laredo, Cardona was the first to join the Zetas, which was a made up of a group of former Mexican Special officers turned drug traffickers, according to Fox News Latino.

In 2004, the 17-year-old was inducted along with 70 other recruits at a Zetas training camp where they were taught combat skills by Colombian mercenaries.

He was then selected to become a sicario, or assassin, for the cartel.

His first job, which landed him on the U.S. law enforcement radar, was a hit on a corrupt Mexican cop who had defected from the Zetas to join rival Sinaloa boss, Chuy Resendez.

Reta, like Cardona, received prison tattoos after his arrest, the only outward indication of what occurred in his past

While most of his crew managed to escape, he was taken to the police station for questioning with Detective Robert Garcia of the Laredo Police Department.

The teen, who was very chatty about his job working for the Zetas, managed to get freed on bond after making up a story for why he was at the murder scene. 

When he returned to Mexico, he had become a commander of the Laredo operation, with an annual income of $1 million, and was able to recruit his own members.

He brought on his pal Reta, who was 13 years old at the time and who seemed to enjoy the bloody work.

Reta, who was practically raised by Zetas leader Miguel Ángel Treviño, earned the highest scores at training camp before he became known as a killing machine, according to the New York Daily News.

During one job, a suspected smuggler's wife and three-year-old child looked on as Reta murdered him in his car.

Reta ended up earning so much in commissions that after winning a $70,000 Mercedes at a cartel party, he handed the keys to Cardona.

In the meantime, Garcia and law enforcement in Laredo had stepped up their efforts to catch Cardona as Reta started calling Garcia threatening him and his family.

As the Zetas prepared to 'slaughter' the top 40 people on Trevino's hit list, a $500,000 bounty was placed on Garcia.

In 2006, Trevino ordered Cardona to assemble a hit squad and prepare for the massacre in which they were to 'slaughter' his top 40 targets in Laredo.

U.S. law enforcement learned of the impending attack from an informant who was responsible for securing a safe house.

Before authorities could wiretap the safe house to learn the names on the hit list, the hit squad found another safe house and authorities were no longer in touch with the informant.

The hit squad also managed to kill one of those on the list, the Sinaloa boss, Resendez after spraying 90 rounds of gunfire into his pickup truck on a highway.

Their short bloody careers came crashing down one after the other, with Cardona sentenced to 80 years behind bars while Reta got 70 years in prison.

Authorities later reconnected with the informant, and they were able to wiretap the safe house without anyone growing suspicious.

Cops eventually tracked Cardona to the safe house where they took him into custody.

Cardona, who estimates he killed between 20 and 30 people, pleaded guilty to the four known murders in the U.S. and was sentenced to 80 years in prison. 

Police have said that one of the crimes for which he is serving time is the kidnap and murder of two American teenagers, during which he slashed them with a bottle, collected their blood in a glass and toasted the La Santa Muerte, the personification of death.

Following his arrest, Laredo U.S. Attorney Angel Moreno said the murder rate in the border town had dropped by half, according to Fox News Latino.

Reta, who is rumored to have committed 30 murders, went rogue from the cartel and killed four when he shot up a nightclub in Monterrey, Mexico.

He is said to have bragged in the interrogation room about feeding live humans to white tigers.

Reta was eventually arrested and extradited to the U.S. where he faced charges and was sentenced to 70 years in prison.

During an interview with CNN in 2013, he said he regret his life of violence.

'I've come to regret everything I've done. I couldn't take it anymore. It was real hard for me. I wasn't living my life,' he said.

Wolf Boys is set to be released on September 13.
