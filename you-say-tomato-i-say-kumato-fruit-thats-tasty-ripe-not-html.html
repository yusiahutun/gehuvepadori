You say tomato, I say kumato... the fruit that's tasty whether it's ripe or not

It may not look particularly appealing, but this tomato could soon be appearing in our summer salads.

While most of us wouldn't dream of consuming a tomato that wasn't red, this one can be eaten at any colour through the ripening process, from dark green onwards.

The Kumato, as it is called, originates from a ‘lost’ wild tomato and has been developed through ten years of cross-breeding by plant specialists Syngenta.

Historically, British consumers would not dream of eating unripened green tomatoes. However, the Kumato is designed to change this perception.

Enlarge  

The mini kumato changes colour over time and is tasty at each stage

When it has a dark green skin, it has a mild clean taste and is good for slicing, it then ripens to a dark brown skin with a sweet spicy aroma, ideal for salads and sandwiches.

Finally when it develops into a dark red, it becomes more juicy and particularly sweet due to a higher fructose level than traditional tomatoes.

The Kumato comes in two sizes, the normal golf-ball size and a mini version.

Sensory scientist at Syngenta, Dr Ian Puddephat, said: 'While Continental Europeans and Americans have eaten multi-coloured tomatoes for years, British consumers have traditionally only bought red.

'Our research reveals that the British palette has now ‘ripened’ and the UK shopper is ready to try new things.'

He said: 'Our mini Kumato is a multi-coloured intense taste sensation that will challenge the British misconception that only ‘red’ tomatoes are ripe, tasty and ready to eat.'

The Kumato has been grown and eaten in Europe, including Spain and France, for a number of years.

Developers claim the young tomatoes, with their dark green skin, have a mild clean taste

Now, it is also being grown in Lancashire where the climate is mild enough to grow the seeds, due to the warm gulfstream passing the Irish Sea.

Syngenta, which is known for its interest in developing GM foods is quick to point out that the Kumato has not been genetically modified in the laboratory.

It said: 'Many consumers, facing for the first time unusual vegetables such as Kumato tomatoes, might be induced to think that it has been obtained via futuristic genetic modification. But we can assure this is not the case.'

The new variety will go on sale through 50 stores in the Marks & Spencer chain from next Monday. A 275g pack of mini Kumato or as 450g pack of the larger variety sell for £2.49.

M&S tomato specialist George Hebditch said: 'The Kumato has an appealing sweet flavour and will offer a really exciting new look to salads.'

Syngeta said the name had been inspired by lyrics from the famous song by George and Ira Gershwin in 1937 called 'Let's Call the Whole Thing Off'.

The song uses a play on words to emphasise the different pronounciation of the word in Britain versus the United States with the phrase: 'You say tomato, I say tomato'.

Syngenta said that kumato was chosen to identify the fact that while there are similarities with the tomato, it is also very different in terms of colour and taste.
